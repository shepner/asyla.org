Pannier

Jun 11th, 2010 12:00 am

This is the first pannier that I had designed some years ago and is the one I use whenever I ride.  Its just the right size to hold my clothes and shoes when commuting to work.  But then why would I design something something that I didnt like…

For features, there is a pocket on either side in convenient locations, and there are 3 flat pockets on the front.  There is also a large pocket inside to hold maps and the like.  The top flap covers the zipper, the straps are intended to remove strain from the zipper and tighten everything up.

The fabric is a coated nylon.  I think it came from  outdoor fabric supply but I forget the name.  The light colored stripes are reflective fabric.  I believe its something from 3M and came from the same supplier.  The webbing is sewn onto the top flap and buckles into pieces sewn into the bottom.  The shoulder strap came from somewhere.  The back is Cordera (backpack cloth) which also covers the bottom of the bag.

The mounting hardware is from Arkel which you can buy separately at a reasonable price.  Arkel has a newer style which is fancier and, IMO, not as easy to use.  It used to be possible to request this older style but I dont know if they still sell it anymore.

Here you can see how that bracket is attached.  The yellow stuff is Coroplat (corrugated plastic) which gives the bracket something to attach to as well as keeps the corners out the spokes.

This photo is showing a little fabric cover hiding the mounting hardware and bungee/hook.  It makes for a cleaner look and keeps the parts from causing too much trouble when carrying the bag.  Prolly should have made the flap a little wider as the ends of the bungee tend to stick out.  Also, the Velcro isnt working as well as it used to.

You may notice that this isnt exactly a waterproof bag.  Thats by design.  The idea is to not worry about waterproof.  Waterproof is heavy, expensive and/or fragile.  Instead, this is intended to control the water which it does very well.  Anything I carry on the bike gets put inside a (generally waterproof) stuffsack or plastic bag anyways so why not design something that takes advantage of that.  The flap over the top does a good job of shedding falling water.  The Coroplast back deflects the water from the wheel.  The bottom is designed to drain water that gets inside.  The only time the insides were truly wet was when the bag got submerged (really deep water that I was dumb enough to ride through) but the contents of the bag still stayed dry.

I should mention that this is the final version of this bag.  There was a prototype made out of (bright yellow) cotton canvas which I had used for a season.  This final version was actually constructed after the big panniers and corrected some of the mistakes.
